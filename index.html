In vector calculus, there are three important operations that are related to $ \nabla, $ the vector differential operator. $ \nabla $ can be operated on both scalar and vector fields.

Scalar Field $ \Omega( \mathbf{r} ) $

A scalar field is a function that outputs a scalar value, given $ n $ independent variables in $ \mathbb{R}^n. $ It assigns a scalar value to every point in space. A nice example of a scalar field is an elevation map as seen on the right. The elevation value, which is a scalar value, depends on the $ x $ and $ y $ coordinate values on the map. Contour lines or colours are usually used to represent the "height" of the function value, as seen in the map. Some other examples of scalar fields are temperature of a room, pressure distribution in a fluid etc.

Vector Field $ \mathbf{f}( \mathbf{r} ) $

A vector field is very similar to a scalar field, except that the function outputs a vector, given $ n $ independent variables in $ \mathbb{R}^n. $ It is used to represent vectorial information. For example, the ocean current map on the right is an example of a vector field that depends on the $ x $ and $ y $ coordinates. At any position, the function assigns a vector that indicates the speed and direction of the ocean current. Other examples of vector fields are electric and magnetic fields.

$ \nabla \Omega, $ $ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{f}, $ $ \nabla \times \mathbf{f} $

In 3D Cartesian coordinates, $ \nabla $ is given by $$ \nabla = \mathbf{\hat{i}} \frac {\partial}{\partial x} + \mathbf{\hat{j}} \frac {\partial}{\partial y} + \mathbf{\hat{k}} \frac {\partial}{\partial z}. $$ $ \nabla $ operates on a scalar field $ \Omega $ in one way, that is: $$ \nabla \Omega . $$ This returns a vector field, which is the gradient field of $ \Omega. $ On the other hand, $ \nabla $ can operate on a vector field $ \mathbf{f} $ in two ways, namely $$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{f} $$ and $$ \nabla \times \mathbf{f}, $$ which give the divergence (scalar) and the curl (vector) of a vector field respectively.

In this visualisation suite, we will be placing our focus on the meaning of grad, divergence and curl instead of their mathematical derivations in the following sections, in order to provide a physical intuition to what $ \nabla $ means, both microscopically and macroscopically.

Gradient Operator: $ \nabla \Omega $

Grad operates on a scalar field $ f(x, y) $ to produce a vector field indicating the magnitude and direction of the steepest gradient at each point.

It is easy to remember that grad produces a vector field, because it is necessary for the gradient to have a direction once there is more than one dimension involved. It must act on a scalar field because it doesn't make much sense for a vector field to have a gradient.

Visualisation Guide

The surface shown on the right is a scalar function $ f $ of two independent variables, $ x $ and $ y. $ Note that it is a 2D scalar field — the third dimension is just being used to display the value of the function.

Beneath it the gradient of the function $ \nabla f $ is shown. By changing the coefficient $ a, $ the gradient of the function can be increased and decreased in certain directions. There are also several functions to choose from.

The idea extends to three dimensions in the same way. For example, if you were to plot the magnitude of the Sun's gravitational field in 3D space around the Sun, the gradient of this field would give vectors that point to the centre of the Sun at every point.
$$ \Omega_B - \Omega_A = \int _A ^B \nabla \Omega \cdot d\mathbf{l} $$ For a given vector field, if $ \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{l} $ is an exact differential, $ \mathbf{A} $ is a conservative field. Then, $ \mathbf{A} $ is the gradient function of some scalar function $ \Omega. $ That is, $ \mathbf{A} = \nabla {\Omega}. $

We can relate $ \Omega $ and $ \nabla \Omega $ by the equation below: $$ \int _A ^B \nabla \Omega \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \Omega_B - \Omega_A $$ From this equation above, we can see that the integral is independent of the path that one takes from A to B, for it is determined by only the values from the parent function $ \Omega_B - \Omega_A. $ This is a generalisation of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Visualisation Guide

  1. On the right, you can see a gradient field and its scalar parent function. Change the value of the parameter $ a, $ and observe the change in $ \nabla \Omega $ and $ \Omega. $

  2. Note that as the magnitude of $ a $ increases, the length of the arrows in $ \nabla \Omega $ increases and the contour of the parent function $ \Omega $ becomes steeper as well. Hence, the gradient vector increases in magnitude as well.

  3. All gradient fields shown in this suite are conservative. This means that the line integral along the field, $ \int \nabla \Omega \cdot d\mathbf{l} $ is path independent. Move the path of the ball from Point A to Point B along 2 different paths, Path 1 and Path 2.

  4. Observe their function values. Note that along the path, the value of $ \Omega $ may be different. However, when the balls reach Point B, their function values are equal.

  5. This means that the line integral $ \int _A ^B \nabla \Omega \cdot d\mathbf{l} $ is determined only by the value of $ \Omega $ at the limits, $ \Omega_B - \Omega_A. $

Divergence Operator: $ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{f} $

Divergence is a vector operator that describes the quantity of a vector field at each point. It is the volume density of outward flux of a vector field at an infinitesimal volume around a point. This leads to another way of understanding divergence:

Imagine a point $ (x_A,y_A) $ in the vector field, which has a vector output assigned to it. If $ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{f}(x_A,y_A) \neq 0, $ as we move along an infinitesimal distance parallel to the direction of the vector at the point (that is $ \mathbf{f}(x_A,y_A)), $ there is a change in the magnitude of the output vector, which is the field strength. If divergence is positive, the field strength increases as we move in the direction of the vector at $ (x_A,y_A). $ If divergence is negative, the field strength decreases as we move in the direction of the vector at the point.

Visualisation Guide

  1. A vector field is shown on the right. This field has a constant divergence value as indicated.

  2. Note that the field does not look like a "source" or "sink". However, the field strength still changes along the x-direction, which indicates a non-zero divergence. As you move along a direction parallel to the vector field at any point, there is a change in the magnitude of the vector field. We can imagine this as if field lines are created or destroyed as we move along.

  3. Adjust the slider until the value of divergence is zero. Note that the length of the arrows in the field are constant; there is no creation or destruction of field lines. Hence, this is a divergence-free field.

Flux - the flow of field lines

We often call $ \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} $ the flux of field $ \mathbf{F} $ perpendicular through a surface element $ d\mathbf{S}. $

The net flux through any surface $ \mathbf{S} $ is given by the integral: $$ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} $$ By considering a closed surface, the Divergence Theorem relates the net flux to the divergence of the field: $$ {\large\bigcirc}\kern-1.55em\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV $$ An example of the Divergence Theorem is Maxwell's Equation.

The Curl Operator: $ \nabla \times \mathbf{f} $

Curl is a vector operator that describes the infinitesimal rotation of a vector field. It can be seen as the tendency of rotation at a point in the vector field. Curl exists in circulation field such as a vortex.

However, curl can still manifest in a non "circulation-looking" vector field like the one on the right. Consider a microscopic point placed in the vector field; if the field strength acting on the point varies at different parts of the point, it creates a tendency for the point to rotate.

This leads to another way of understanding curl:

Imagine travelling over an infinitesimal distance in the direction perpendicular to the vector field at a point. If the field strength changes along this infinitesimal distance travelled, there is a non-zero curl at the point.

Visualisation Guide

  1. A vector field is shown on the right. This field has a curl value as indicated.

  2. Note that the field does not look like a "circulation". However, as you move along a direction perpendicular to the vector field at any point, there is a change in the magnitude of the vector field. this gives a paddle the tendency to rotate in such a field.

  3. Adjust the slider until the value of curl is zero. Note that the length of the arrows in the field are constant; there is no rotational tendency, only translation. Hence, this is a curl-free field.

Stokes' Theorem states: $$ \oint_{l} \mathbf {B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \iint_{S} \nabla \times \mathbf {B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} $$ i.e. the closed loop integral of a vector field is equal to the sum of all the curl contained in the surface enclosed by the loop.

This is demonstrated in the visualisation on the right, where the components of $ \mathbf {B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} $ as you go around the loop are shown in the plot at the bottom. The contributions are positive if the field and dl are pointing in the same direction.

Visualisation Guide

When there is curl inside the loop then the overall loop integral is non-zero, since on all parts of the loop, the contributions to the integral from the curl source have the same sign; however when the curl source is outside of the loop, then the contributions cancel out and the net integral is zero.

This also demonstrates that the curl of a conservative field is zero, since any loop integral in a conservative field is also zero.

You can change the shape of the loop to see that this is true for any type of closed loop.

In vector calculus, there are three important operations that are related to $ \nabla, $ the vector differential operator.

The three operations are gradient $ \nabla, $ divergence $ \nabla \cdot $ and curl $ \nabla \times. $ With these operations, we can specify any scalar field or vector field if given enough initial conditions.

To specify a scalar field, we need the value at a point and gradient of the entire space. To specify a vector field, we need the value at a point along with the divergence and curl of the entire space.

There are two theorems that connect these differential operations to integration. They are the Divergence Theorem: $$ {\large\bigcirc}\kern-1.55em\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV $$ and Stoke's Theorem: $$ \oint_{l} \mathbf{A.dl} = \displaystyle \iint_{S} \nabla \times \mathbf {A} \cdot d\mathbf{S} $$ Vector calculus is used extensively in electromagnetic fields, gravitational fields and fluid flow.
