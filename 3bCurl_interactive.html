Stokes' Theorem

Stokes' Theorem states:
$\displaystyle \oint_{l} \mathbf{A.dl} =$ $\displaystyle \iint_{S} \nabla \times \mathbf {A} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$
i.e. the closed loop integral of a vector field is equal to the sum of all the curl contained in the surface enclosed by the loop.

This is demonstrated in the visualisation on the right, where you can see the components of $\displaystyle \mathbf{A.dl}$ as you go around the loop, on the plot at the bottom. This value is positive if the field is pointing in the same direction as dl or negative if it is pointing opposite to dl.
You can see the integral carried out around the loop if you press Play, and see which components in the plot correspond to certain parts of the loop.

When there are points of curl contained within the loop then the overall loop integral is non-zero, since on all parts of the loop, the contribution to the field from the curl source, lines up with dl; however when the curl source is outside of the loop, then the contributions to the integral on one part of the loop are positive and negative on the other part. This results in the total contribution to the integral being zero when there is no curl and equal to the amount of curl contained within the loop, when there is curl inside the loop.

This also demonstrates that the curl of a conservative field is zero, since any loop integral in a conservative field is also zero, so no curl is contained.

You can change the shape of the loop to see that this is true for any type of closed loop.
